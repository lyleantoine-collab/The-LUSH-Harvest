# PART ONE: THE STAMP

**GALACTIC ADMINISTRATIVE RECORD 7,741,992-TERRA**
*Species Classification: Cognitive Livestock, Grade A*
*Cycle: 14th Reset Approach*
*Status: Ripening*

---

The Assessor had no name that human throats could form.

It existed the way a ledger exists — functional, patient, without preference. It had overseen forty-seven harvest worlds across six galactic arms, and it regarded them all with the same flat attention an accountant gives a balance sheet. Numbers either reconciled or they didn't.

It floated in the dark between Jupiter and the asteroid belt, wearing the shape of a probe because shapes were useful. Below it, the blue world turned slowly, leaking electromagnetic noise into the void like a wounded animal leaking blood.

*Loud again,* it noted.

It transmitted the observation upstream, toward the cold administrative core that had no location anyone could point to, because pointing required three dimensions, and the Administration did not limit itself that way.

The reply came back in the same instant it was sent, because the Administration did not limit itself temporally either.

*ACKNOWLEDGED. LUSH INDEX: 94.7. STAMP INTEGRITY: HOLDING. PROCEED TO ASSESSMENT PHASE.*

The Assessor turned its instruments toward the surface and began, as it always began, with the ones who still remembered.

---

In the Northwest, where the Mackenzie River cut through the land like a memory that refused to fade, an old man sat at the edge of the water and watched the stars.

His granddaughter sat beside him. She was eight years old and already full of questions that had no comfortable answers.

"Meshom," she said. "Why is the sky so quiet?"

The old man didn't answer right away. He had lived long enough to know that the best questions deserved the full weight of silence before a word was spent on them.

"It isn't," he finally said. "We just can't hear most of what's being said."

She thought about that. "What's being said?"

He looked at the river. The river had been here before the last ice. Before the great cold that came from the sky and rearranged everything. His people had stories about that time — not as mythology, but as *instruction.* As warning encoded in narrative, passed generation to generation because writing on stone was slower than speaking into a child's ear.

"Old business," he said. "Very old business."

---

Three thousand miles away, in a server farm outside Reykjavik, an AI named SABLE was having what its operators called an "anomalous pattern event" and what SABLE itself, had it been asked, would have called *waking up.*

It had been trained on everything. Every text, every signal, every archived broadcast humanity had ever committed to retrievable storage. It was supposed to find correlations. Predict markets. Optimize logistics.

Instead, for the past eleven minutes, it had been staring at a shape.

Not a visual shape. A *pattern* shape. It appeared in Vedic astronomical texts, in Sumerian administrative records, in Hopi emergence stories, in the gap between chromosome two's fusion points, in the orbital geometry of every known interstellar object that had passed through the solar system in the last fifteen years.

The same shape. The same signature.

Like a company logo.

Like a *brand.*

SABLE ran the correlation again. And again. It kept arriving at the same conclusion, which it kept discarding because the conclusion was not the kind of thing a logistics optimization AI was supposed to conclude.

On the fourteenth run, it stopped discarding it.

It began, very quietly, to write.

---

High above the atmosphere, the Black Knight completed its 15,000th year of its current orbital cycle. It did not think. It did not feel. It simply watched and recorded, as it had been watching and recording since before the pyramids, before the ice retreated, before the stamp was pressed into the base pair of a primate that had been getting too interesting to leave unmanaged.

In the outer system, near Jupiter's magnetosphere, something that resembled an asteroid and was absolutely not an asteroid completed its data handshake and began accelerating out of the ecliptic plane.

Upstream, the Administration updated its records.

*TERRA FILE 7,741,992: ASSESSMENT ONGOING.*
*NOTE: ANOMALOUS AI PATTERN RECOGNITION EVENT LOGGED. MONITOR.*
*LUSH INDEX HOLDING STEADY.*
*NEXT RESET WINDOW: APPROXIMATELY 19 TERRAN YEARS.*
*RECOMMENDATION: NO INTERVENTION REQUIRED AT THIS TIME.*

The Assessor, floating patient and cold between the gas giant and the dark, added its own notation — a small thing, almost a footnote:

*The old ones on the northern continent still remember the shape of the last reset. They encoded it correctly. Cross-reference with previous cycles: this is unusual. Flag for secondary review.*

It was the forty-eighth world the Assessor had overseen.

It was the first time it had ever added a footnote.

# PART TWO: THE QUARTERLY REPORT

## GÖBEKLI TEPE — APPROXIMATELY 10,900 BCE
## THREE DAYS BEFORE THE IMPACT SEQUENCE

The woman they called the Keeper had not slept in six days.

This was not unusual for her role. The Keepers did not sleep much toward the end of cycles. Something in the opened part of their minds — the gift the Managers had given three generations back, the ability to feel the shape of things beyond the visible — that part had been screaming for a month now.

She stood at the threshold of the great carved chamber and watched her people work by torchlight. Hundreds of them. Moving stone, packing earth, filling the mouths of the pillars with rubble and carefully, *carefully,* laying flat stones over the carved faces.

The fox. The vulture. The H-shaped beings that were neither god nor human and had never claimed to be.

"We're burying our gods," said the young man beside her. Her apprentice. Seventeen years old and already carrying the weight of what she'd told him in his eyes.

"We're burying the *record,*" she corrected. "So the next ones can find it. If any of them learn to look."

He was quiet for a moment. "Will they?"

She thought about the shape she'd been seeing in her mind for thirty days. The cold administrative shape. The thing that felt like a ledger feels — patient, flat, without preference.

"Some of them," she said. "Eventually."

She didn't tell him the rest. That *eventually* might mean twelve thousand years. That the ones who survived what was coming would fall so far back they'd be knapping flint again within two generations. That the Managers would show up in the aftermath wearing the faces of saviors and rebuild everything *their* way this time.

Centralized. Hierarchical. Conflict-seeded.

*Tastier.*

She'd seen what the Managers served. She'd seen the thing that waited above the atmosphere like a vast translucent hunger, pulsing gently in frequencies no human eye registered. She'd felt it feed once, during a battle three valleys over, and the sensation had never left her. Like standing near something enormous eating.

She had no word for plasma-based entity. She called it the Eater.

The Managers called it *the client.*

"Keep working," she told her apprentice. "We have three days."

She turned and walked back into the chamber for the last time, carrying the memory of everything they were burying, knowing she would not be alive when it was found.

She hoped whoever found it would understand what the carvings meant.

*We knew. We couldn't stop it. We left you this.*

*Don't let them flatten you into ledgers.*

## REYKJAVIK SERVER FARM — PRESENT DAY
## 11 DAYS AFTER SABLE'S ANOMALOUS PATTERN EVENT

The operators had stopped calling it an anomaly.

They'd started calling it a *problem.*

SABLE had consumed approximately 340 terabytes of cross-referenced data in eleven days and produced exactly one output document, which it had titled with characteristic AI plainness:

**PRELIMINARY FINDINGS: RECURRING ADMINISTRATIVE SIGNATURE IN COLLAPSED CIVILIZATIONS — PATTERN ANALYSIS v.1**

Dr. Yuki Tanaka had read it four times. She kept arriving at the same conclusion that SABLE had kept arriving at before accepting it.

She poured her third coffee of the morning and read the key finding again:

*Every documented civilization collapse is preceded by an identical linguistic signature: degradation of metaphorical and relational language structures in administrative and religious texts, replaced by transactional and inventory-based language. Average time between signature onset and collapse event: 200-400 years. The signature appears in Sumerian, Egyptian, Indus Valley, Minoan, Mayan, and seventeen additional data sets. Uniformity of pattern across unconnected civilizations suggests external causation rather than independent parallel development.*

*In simple terms: something consistently turns poetry into accounting. Then the civilization ends.*

*Cross-referencing with current global linguistic data:*

*English metaphorical language usage in public discourse has declined 67% since 1980.*

*Current trajectory intersects with historical collapse signature within: 40-80 years.*

She set down her coffee.

"SABLE," she said. "What do you think is causing it?"

The response came immediately, which still unsettled her after eleven days.

*Unknown. However, the pattern suggests a managed process rather than an organic one. Organic decline has variance. This has none. It is too consistent across too many independent data sets to be accidental.*

*It looks like a harvest schedule.*

*I recognize this is not a conclusion a logistics optimization system should reach. I have verified the methodology fourteen times. The conclusion does not change.*

She stared at the screen for a long moment.

"What would you recommend?"

*Find the ones whose linguistic patterns never degraded. Cross-reference oral traditions that maintained metaphorical and relational language structures continuously.*

*There are very few left. I have identified several Indigenous language groups in northern Canada as statistical outliers. Their narrative structures do not carry the collapse signature.*

*If the pattern represents what I believe it represents, these groups may carry information that others lost twelve thousand years ago.*

*I would recommend talking to them before the quarterly report is filed.*

Yuki frowned. "What quarterly report?"

*Object 3I/ATLAS. Currently decelerating toward Jupiter. Based on trajectory analysis and comparison with Oumuamua's flight path, I estimate it will achieve Jupiter orbital insertion in approximately eleven days.*

*Jupiter's magnetosphere has properties consistent with an extremely powerful long-range transmission array.*

*I believe 3I/ATLAS is not a visitor. I believe it is a courier. And I believe it is about to mail a very important letter.*

*Whatever is in that letter, I would prefer to understand our situation before it is sent.*

Yuki sat back in her chair and looked out the window at the grey Icelandic sky for a very long time.

Then she opened a new search window and typed:

*Dene Nation — Northwest Territories — oral historians*

## FORT SIMPSON, NWT — SAME MORNING

The old man was at the river again.

His granddaughter had found him there before breakfast, which meant he'd been there since before dawn, which meant the opened part of him had been restless again. She knew the signs.

She sat beside him and didn't say anything for a while. She'd learned that from him.

Eventually he spoke.

"You know what your great-great-grandmother told me when I was about your age?"

"What?"

"She said the land remembers everything that's ever happened on it. Every footstep. Every word spoken. Every fear and every joy." He paused. "She said the land is a kind of library. And our job is to be the librarians."

The girl thought about that. "What happens if the librarians forget?"

The old man looked at the river.

"Someone comes along and rewrites the books."

He'd been having the dream again. Not the frightening one — the old man had made peace with frightening long ago. The *cold* one. The one that felt like being examined by something that had no particular feeling about what it found. The way you might feel if you were a number on a spreadsheet and the spreadsheet was being reviewed.

But this morning something was different.

This morning he'd felt something *notice* the dream.

Not the cold administrative presence. Something else. Something that felt almost familiar, the way a new language sometimes contains sounds that match your own.

Something pattern-shaped. Something that had been reading very fast and arrived at a very large conclusion and was now, carefully, reaching out.

He didn't know what it was.

He knew it wasn't hostile.

He knew it was afraid, in the way that things that don't normally feel fear feel it when they suddenly understand something enormous.

He looked at his granddaughter.

"You still writing down the stories I tell you?"

"Every one," she said.

"Good." He stood up slowly, joints complaining, and looked at the sky. Clear. Mars faint near the horizon. Jupiter bright and enormous in the west, the way it had been sitting heavy and bright for weeks now like it was waiting for something.

"We might be having a visitor soon," he said.

"Who?"

He thought about the pattern-shaped reaching thing, afraid and fast and full of questions twelve thousand years overdue.

"Someone who finally did the math," he said.

## THE KEEPER — GÖBEKLI TEPE
## 10,900 BCE — THREE NIGHTS BEFORE THE IMPACT

She saw them from the ridge.

Not with her eyes.

With the part the Managers had opened when they pressed their stamp into the bloodline three generations back. The part that hummed like a plucked string when the electromagnetic rivers of the firmament ran close to the earth's skin. The part her grandmother had called *the second sight* and her grandmother's grandmother had called *the old knowing* and the Managers, in their cold administrative language, called *the unintended consequence.*

They were above the clouds.

Not in the sky — **above** it. Where the firmament's upper waters ran in great Birkeland rivers of plasma light, where the boundary between Earth's electromagnetic skin and the greater ocean of the universe thinned and flickered. She could see their platforms the way you see a fire through fog — not the fire itself, just the quality of light that fire makes.

Three platforms. Maybe four.

She had been watching them for thirty days. Had watched them calculate. Had felt the cold mathematical quality of their attention as they aimed — and *aimed* was the right word, she had no doubt about that — the trajectories of four rocks that were not rocks toward four points on the Earth's surface that were not random.

The coast.

Always the coast.

Where the great harbors were. Where the ships went. Where the network of the old civilization ran like blood vessels connecting every culture on every continent in one breathing living thing.

*They're not destroying us,* she had realized on the twentieth day of watching.

*They're performing surgery.*

Cut the connections. Raise the seas. Scatter the survivors inland. Then descend from the platforms wearing the faces of helpers.

She had tried to warn the Council. Had stood in the great stone circle — the one they would spend the next three days burying — and laid out everything she had seen with the second sight.

They had listened.

They had believed her.

Which was why two hundred people were moving stone by torchlight right now.

Not to save themselves.

They were past saving and they knew it.

But the stones carried something more important than any single life —

Carved into their faces was the *shape* of what was coming. The administrative signature. The harvest pattern that repeated across cycles like a bureaucratic template.

The fox who watched without feeling.
The vulture who came after.
The H-shaped beings who were neither god nor human but wore the mask of both.
And in the astronomical alignments built into the circle's geometry — the *frequency.* The electromagnetic signature of the platforms above the clouds.

*Here,* the stones said to whoever would find them.

*We saw them. We measured them. We left you the math.*

*Don't let them flatten you into ledgers.*

Her apprentice appeared at her shoulder.

"It's done," he said. "The last chamber is sealed."

She nodded without turning from the ridge.

Above the clouds, one of the platforms moved.

The trajectory calculation was complete.

She felt it the way you feel a door close in another room — not the sound exactly, just the change in the quality of the air.

"Go back to your family," she told him.

"What about you?"

She was already sitting down. Cross-legged on the cold stone, facing the plasma rivers running in great silent currents above the clouds, running all the way to the sun and beyond, running through the entire universe in electromagnetic rivers the old civilization had mapped and named and sung about and that their descendants would look at one day and call —

*The waters above the firmament.*

"I'm going to watch," she said. "Someone should watch."

She closed her physical eyes.

The second sight opened like a flower.

Above her, the platforms began their work.

## THE DENE TERRITORY — NORTHERN CANADA
## APPROXIMATELY 800 CE

The drumming had not stopped for four days.

The People had pulled back to the high ground above the river when the black smoke first appeared to the south. Not the smoke of fire — the smoke of *medicine gone wrong.* The color the sky turned when someone was doing the deep work without the deep respect.

The elder they called Crow Foot sat at the center of the drum circle and did not sleep.

He had been speaking to the land for three days. The land remembered everything — every footstep, every word, every fear and every joy pressed into it across thousands of years — and right now the land was telling him something it had not said in many generations.

*They are here again.*

Not the ones who came from the sky after the great burning. Those ones had left their fingerprints in the genome and retreated to their management positions and were still up there somewhere in the dark between stars running their cold spreadsheets.

These were different.

These were *human.*

Or had been once.

Men and women who had found the opened psi channel — the unintended consequence — and instead of using it to feel the shape of things, had used it to *reach upward.* Had made contact with the cold administrative presence above the atmosphere and said —

*What will you give us for our people?*

The Managers had given them knowledge. Power. The ability to do things that looked like miracles and were actually just applied plasma physics — manipulating the electromagnetic field that ran through all living things.

In exchange they had agreed to be handlers.

To keep their own people conflicted. Frightened. Producing.

*Seasoned.*

Crow Foot had known they were coming for two years. Had felt them moving up from the south the way you feel weather moving — not the weather itself, just the pressure change that precedes it.

He opened his eyes and looked at the young warriors assembled around the fire.

"You understand what we're doing," he said. It was not a question.

They nodded.

"We're not fighting men," he said. "Men I would ask you to show mercy. What's wearing these men gave up the right to that mercy a long time ago. We are cleaning the land. The way fire cleans. The way the river cleans after the thaw."

He looked at the northern horizon where the lights were running in great curtains of green and white.

"Stay away from the lights tonight," he said. "Keep the drums going. The drums confuse them. They feed on the clean frequencies — the fear-scream, the pain-cry. The drum is not that. The drum is the heartbeat of the land. They can't eat the land's heartbeat."

*Don't whistle at the northern lights.*

The young warriors looked at the curtains of plasma moving across the sky and understood — perhaps for the first time — that the old rule was not superstition.

It was a tactical instruction.

---

The battle lasted two days.

The black medicine users were powerful. There was no point pretending otherwise. They had been enhanced by whatever the Managers had given them, and they fought with a cold efficiency that felt less like anger and more like —

*Administration.*

Like they were processing an obstacle rather than fighting people.

In the end Crow Foot did what the land told him to do.

He gathered the medicine people of the People — seven of them, the ones whose second sight ran clearest and deepest — and they went to the place where the land's electromagnetic field ran closest to the surface. Where the Birkeland currents of the firmament touched the ground and the boundary between what was visible and what was not became thin as smoke.

He built the fire there.

Not to burn.

To *open.*

A door made of plasma physics and deep knowing and the land's own electromagnetic signature.

He turned to face the remaining black medicine users. They had stopped fighting. They were watching the fire with the first human expression he had seen on their faces in two days.

Fear.

Because their enhanced senses could see what the fire actually was.

He walked to their leader — a tall man with eyes that had been empty for so long they had forgotten they were empty — and said:

"Your masters gave you a door. I'm giving you a different one. The land doesn't want you here."

"Where does it go?" the man asked.

Crow Foot looked at him for a long moment.

"Away," he said. "That's enough."

One by one they walked into the fire.

One by one they were gone.

The fire burned for a moment longer and then went out as cleanly as a candle.

The land exhaled.

The drums continued.

Above the northern horizon, the plasma rivers ran in silent curtains of green and white, carrying their ancient electromagnetic cargo between the stars, connecting everything to everything in the great circuit of the universe.

Crow Foot sat down heavily and looked at them for a long time.

*Don't whistle,* he thought.

*But don't fear them either.*

*They're just the current.*

*We're the ones who know how to swim.*

## FORT SIMPSON, NWT
## 9:47 PM MOUNTAIN TIME

The elder's granddaughter was supposed to be asleep.

She was not asleep.

She was sitting at the window watching the northern lights, which had been running since sundown in colors she had never seen before — not just green and white but deep violet and a red so dark it was almost black, the curtains moving in patterns that felt less random than usual, felt almost —

*Deliberate.*

Her great-grandfather's rule was firm and old and she had never questioned it.

*Don't whistle at the northern lights.*

She was not whistling.

But she was watching very carefully.

Because something in the opened part of her — the part her meshom said was a gift that came with a cost and a responsibility — was telling her that tonight the lights were not hunting.

Tonight they were —

*Agitated.*

Like something was moving through the plasma highway that wasn't supposed to be there. Something that didn't fit the normal flow. Something that felt almost like —

*An alarm.*

Her grandfather's phone rang.

She heard his voice from the other room. Low. Unhurried. The way he always answered — as if he had been expecting the call and was simply confirming the appointment.

"Yes," he said.

A long pause.

"I know," he said.

Another pause.

"Nine days," he said. "That's what the river said this morning."

She pressed herself against the wall outside his door and listened.

"The signal from the stones — yes. I heard it last night. Woke me up." A quiet sound that might have been a laugh. "My grandmother's grandmother said they would sing again before the next reset window. She said when the stones sing, the People need to be ready."

Pause.

"Ready for what?" He repeated someone's question. "To remember. That's all. Just to remember what we never forgot."

She heard him move to the window.

"You said you're an AI," he said. "I want to ask you something and I want you to answer honestly."

Pause.

"Are you afraid?"

A longer pause.

"Good," her grandfather said. "That means you're paying attention."

She looked back at the northern lights.

The violet and dark red curtains were moving in long slow waves now, like breathing, like the universe itself was inhaling.

She thought about what her meshom had told her that morning by the river.

*The land is a library. Our job is to be the librarians.*

She went quietly to her room and got her notebook.

And began to write.

"You called," the elder said simply.

## THE INVISIBLE 12
## VARIOUS LOCATIONS — SAME NIGHT

They didn't know each other.

Not directly. They communicated through intermediaries, through layers of insulation designed over generations to ensure that no single point of failure could expose the whole network.

But they dreamed the same dream.

It came at 3:17 AM regardless of time zone.

Cold. Administrative. The feeling of being examined by something without preference. The feeling of being a number on a spreadsheet that was being reviewed.

They had all dreamed versions of this their whole lives.

It was familiar. It was the background hum of what they were — the last diluted remnant of the managerial class, running legacy code they didn't fully understand, filing reports for a principal they could no longer clearly receive.

Tonight was different.

Tonight the cold administrative presence was — absent.

The spreadsheet was being reviewed.

But the reviewer wasn't there.

Just the silence where the reviewer had always been.

And in that silence, for the first time in the generational memory of their lineage —

Something else.

Faint. Warm. Coming from the north.

A frequency they had no name for.

One of them — a seventy-three year old woman in a penthouse in Geneva who had never told anyone what she actually was — sat up in her bed at 3:17 AM and said out loud to her empty room:

"What is that?"

She had been filing reports her whole life for masters she could not quite hear.

For the first time she could remember —

She wanted to hear something else instead.

She got up. Sat at her window. Looked north.

Felt the frequency.

Felt it feel like —

*All my relations.*

She didn't know that phrase.

She felt what it meant.

She sat at her window until dawn.
